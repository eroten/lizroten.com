StreetLight Data

# Overview

What is StreetLight Data?

–

  • StreetLight Data is a transportation analytics firm that provides access to aggregated location-based services (LBS) data

–

  • The data is anonymized, aggregated, and accessed only through specific analyses

–

How does government use this data?

–

  • The data are widely used in transportation planning by MNDOT, the Minnesota Federal Highway Administration, county governments, local municipalities, and consulting firms.

Why use Streetlight for parks research?

–

  • StreetLight has very large sample sizes

–

  • StreetLight provides data significant at the park level

–

  • StreetLight’s algorithms ensure that we are never able to identify a single person or specific group of people.

  • average_daily_zone_traffic_st_l_index is a relative measure of traffic (activity) in a park

Task 2: Calculate local and non-local visitation

Why do we care?

  • Regional parks are expected to get visitors from the entire region.

–

  • If a park doesn’t have enough non-local visitation, is it a regional park?

Locality according to state statute

A local visitor resides in the park’s agency jurisdiction.

A non-local visitor resides outside the park’s agency jurisdiction.

–

For example,
The City of St. Paul manages Como Regional Park.
If a visitor who resides in St. Paul visits Como, they are .green[local]
If a visitor who resides in Minneapolis visits Como, they are .red[non-local]

Analysis parks and implementing agencies

Categorize block groups by agency area

  • Every block group in the nation needs to have an agency designation.

  • assign agency based on block group centroid

–

Agency categories

  • if a block group is outside the state of MN -> “Nation”

–

  • else if a block groups is outside the metro -> “Greater MN”
- else assign agency by block group centroid

Already done!

load("data/state_agency_block_group.rda")

Join block group data

home_block_group_join <- dplyr::left_join(home_block_group_traffic, 
                                          state_agency_block_group,
                                          by = c("block_group_id" = "GEOID")) %>% 
  rowwise() %>% 
  mutate(AGENCY = case_when(is.na(AGENCY) ~ "Nation",
                            TRUE ~ AGENCY))

Calculate Como locality

como_locality <- home_block_group_join %>% 
  dplyr::filter(zone_name == "Como",
                day_type == "0: All Days (M-Su)",
                day_part == "0: All Day (12am-12am)",
                intersection_type == "Trip End") %>% 
  group_by(AGENCY)  %>% 
  summarize(total_percent = sum(as.numeric(as.character(percent_by_home_location))))

What other ways can we classify locality?

  • Does the visitor live within walking distance? Biking distance?

–

  • What is the definition of these distances?

–

  • Do you measure this distance from the center of the park, or from the nearest boundary?

–

  • Do you take sidewalks and road networks into account?

–

  • Does the visitor live in the metro area?

–

  • Do we use the 7-county metro or the 13-county metro?

–

  • Can Wisconsin residents be considered local?

Discussion + Exploration questions

  • How do different locality measurements benefit the agency?

  • How does trip intersection type influence locality?

  • What distance thresholds make sense for different traffic types (pedestrian, bike, vehicle)?
